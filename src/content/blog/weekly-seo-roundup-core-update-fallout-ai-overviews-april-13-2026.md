Google gave marketers a very clear message this week: clean up risky user experience patterns, be careful with how you read reporting, and start preparing for a search landscape where AI visibility becomes easier to measure.

For small businesses, this is less about chasing every headline and more about tightening the basics that actually hold up after updates.

## 1. Google adds a spam policy for back button hijacking

**Source:** [Google Search Central](https://developers.google.com/search/blog/2026/04/back-button-hijacking)

Google formally added **back button hijacking** to its spam policies. That covers pages or scripts that interfere with normal browser navigation, prevent users from going back, or send them somewhere unexpected. Enforcement starts on **June 15, 2026**.

**Why this matters:**

This is the strongest practical headline of the week because it is not theoretical. If your site uses aggressive ad scripts, popups, engagement widgets, or third-party tools that create deceptive navigation behavior, you now have a clear compliance deadline.

**Practical takeaway:**
- Audit popups, overlays, ad tech, and third-party scripts
- Check mobile behavior carefully, not just desktop
- Remove anything that manipulates browser history or creates a misleading path for users

## 2. Google's March 2026 core update is done, so the analysis window starts now

**Source:** [Search Engine Journal](https://www.searchenginejournal.com/seo-pulse-core-update-done-gsc-bug-fixed-mueller-on-gurus/571626/) and [Search Engine Roundtable](https://www.seroundtable.com/google-march-2026-core-update-complete-41145.html)

Google's March 2026 broad core update finished rolling out on **April 8**. That means this week is the first useful moment to compare pre-update and post-update performance without guessing mid-rollout.

**Why this matters:**

If rankings moved, this is the time to look for patterns by page type, search intent, and content quality. Random rewrites usually make things worse.

**Practical takeaway:**
- Compare winners and losers at the page and query level in Search Console
- Look for pages with weak intent match, thin differentiation, or trust gaps

## 3. Search Console impression data may have been inflated for months

**Source:** [Search Engine Journal](https://www.searchenginejournal.com/seo-pulse-core-update-done-gsc-bug-fixed-mueller-on-gurus/571626/)

Google confirmed a Search Console logging issue that **over-reported impressions starting May 13, 2025**. Clicks and other metrics were not affected, but impression reporting may shift downward as the fix rolls out.

**Why this matters:**

A lot of marketing reporting leans too heavily on impressions. If you have been showing strong visibility growth in dashboards or client reports, some of that lift may have been data noise rather than real performance.

**Practical takeaway:**
- Treat impression trends since May 13, 2025 with caution
- Use clicks, conversions, and revenue as the cleaner performance signal
- Add an annotation to reporting so future comparisons do not get misread

## 4. Google appears to be testing an AI contribution report in Search Console

**Source:** [Search Engine Roundtable](https://www.seroundtable.com/google-search-console-ai-contribution-41153.html)

Google appears to be testing an **AI contribution** area in Search Console. Details are limited, but the early signal suggests Google may eventually give site owners a clearer view into how their pages contribute to AI search experiences.

**Why this matters:**

For marketers, this could become one of the most useful AI-era reporting updates yet. Right now, many teams can feel AI search impact without being able to measure it cleanly.

**Practical takeaway:**
- Track which pages are most likely to earn citations or mentions in AI-generated answers
- Strengthen topic clusters and supporting pages so your best content is easier to trust and cite
- If your site structure is weak, use the [Internal Link Recommender](/products/internal-link-recommender) to tighten internal linking

## 5. AI Overviews still have accuracy and grounding problems

**Source:** [Search Engine Roundtable](https://www.seroundtable.com/times-reports-ai-overviews-are-inaccurate-41152.html)

A new study discussed by The New York Times found that while many AI Overviews were directionally accurate, a meaningful share still had grounding issues, meaning the cited sources did not fully support the claims being made.

**Why this matters:**

This is a reminder that visibility in AI search is not just about being mentioned. It is about making your content easy to verify, easy to summarize, and strong enough to support the claims AI systems might pull from it.

**Practical takeaway:**
- Make key claims explicit on the page instead of implied
- Add examples, evidence, and clear summaries that can stand on their own
